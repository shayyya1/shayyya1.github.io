Passion

My first real dive into computational thinking started through playing Minecraft. I had recently learnt about logic gates in school, and in Minecraft there is redstone which functions the same way. It quickly became a major project of mine to create an addition calculator, completely from first principles, out of logic gates. I spent months theorising and prototyping different solutions. I loved the process of continually developing my knowledge of the problem, and expanding my toolbox through each failed prototype. To illustrate the scale of this project, I've included a short video of my world.

I started small with a binary half adder, then scaled up to n-bit addition by chaining half adders together, which was easy enough. The real challenge came with integrating denary into the interface. In particular, converting the binary outputs to denary was a tremendously difficult problem. I eventually created a steady-state based iterative solution that converts binary to BCD. It does this by separating each binary digit into denary components, and summing these across all digits. For example, 11000000 breaks down into 128 + 64, which we then further break down into one 100, two 10s and six 10s, an eight and a four. We sum across each denary place value to get one 100, eight 10s, and twelve. We then feed this output back into the system, so the twelve becomes one 10 and two, and so on. Eventually a steady-state is reached and the BCD representation is obtained.

My next major personal project was a program designed to solve the classic game Snake. I wrote this in Processing (a Java-based language), and it was my introduction to OOP. I reframed the problem as finding a path from the snake's head to its tail, which goes through every cell in the grid. After extensive research and problem-solving, I created a recursive update procedure which I combined with A* pathfinding. This resulted in a 70% success rate, far exceeding my manual attempts. The key skills I gained during this project which can be applied to Bending Spoons are independent research and creative thinking. I reduced the problem to one found in theory, and researched papers which gave insight and solutions. I then adopted and reapplied these solutions within the context of my program, a process which is applicable throughout all facets of software engineering.

The process of finding a path which goes through all cells exactly once is called a Hamiltonian path, and these are guaranteed to solve Snake as you cannot double back on yourself. I combined a decision procedure for finding Hamiltonian paths with A* pathfinding, where the program would prioritise paths that led towards food. However, finding Hamiltonian paths is NP-Complete, so there is no efficient solution. I instead created a recursive update procedure that can eliminate moves which are not on Hamiltonian paths, but was not guaranteed to eliminate all invalid moves. In the video below you can see the update procedure in action. The black cells are the body of the snake, and the red is the head. All the other coloured states around the snake describe how many valid moves are available from that cell.
